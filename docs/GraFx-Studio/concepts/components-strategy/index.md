# Components — design studio perspective

Components are not just a feature for template designers. They are a structural decision about how your studio organises work, governs brand output, and delivers campaigns to local teams.

This page is written for design studio managers and team leads — people who are less focused on how to click through the component workspace and more focused on what changes about how their studio operates when components are part of the workflow.

---

## The template factory model — and its limits

Most design studios start the same way. A campaign brief comes in. A designer builds a template. The template gets sent to local teams — store managers, regional marketers, field reps — who fill it in and produce their materials.

This works. Until the brand updates. Or until the same campaign runs in twelve regions. Or until HQ needs to add a new product to the leaflet that already went to forty store owners.

In the template-by-template model, every one of those situations means opening templates one by one and making the same change in the same place across every file. The more templates exist, the more that maintenance burden grows. And the more local teams are waiting on HQ to deliver an update before they can run their campaign.

---

## The shift: from production to assembly

The fundamental change that components introduce is a split between **building design elements** and **assembling templates**.

In a component-based studio, the design team builds reusable elements — a pricing block, a product badge, a promotional label, a header — as components. Templates become assemblies of those components, connected to variable data. Local teams get templates that are ready to use, with the brand locked in by design, not by policy.

This changes what your team spends time on. Less rebuilding the same element for every campaign. More building a design library that compounds in value over time.

It also changes the conversation with local teams. Instead of delivering a template for every campaign variant, the studio delivers the component once. Local teams — or the templates themselves — handle the variation.

---

## The investment model

Building a component takes more time than placing the same element directly in a template. That is true, and it is worth planning for.

The payback comes from the second use. And the third. And when the brand updates and you change one file instead of forty.

A useful way to think about it:

| Approach | First template | Second template | Brand update |
|---|---|---|---|
| Element built directly in templates | Fast | Same time again | Update every template |
| Element built as a component | Slower (build component + template) | Fast (reuse component) | Update component once |

The crossover point — where the component approach has saved more time than it cost — typically comes sooner than expected, because brand updates and campaign variants are more frequent than they appear at planning time.

---

## What to componentize

Not every design element belongs in a component. The question is where the value of reuse and central ownership is high enough to justify the upfront build.

**Strong candidates:**

An element is worth building as a component when it appears across more than one template — pricing blocks, promo badges, product ad layouts — or when it appears multiple times within the same template, like a coupon element repeated six times on a sheet. The more frequently it is used, the faster the investment pays back.

An element is also a strong candidate when brand governance matters. If local teams or operators must never be able to change the design of a particular element — only the data it shows — a component enforces that boundary structurally. The local user fills in variables; the design is inaccessible to them.

**Weaker candidates:**

Elements that are unique to one template and will never be reused are simpler to build directly. The overhead of creating a component is not justified by a single use.

---

## Brand governance by design

In a traditional workflow, brand governance is maintained through guidelines, approvals, and training. Local teams are told what they can and cannot change. Enforcement depends on people following the rules.

Components change the enforcement model. A component's internal design cannot be edited from the template. When a local team member — or anyone using the template in Run Mode — interacts with a component, they see only the variables exposed to them. The typography, the layout, the colour usage, the business logic: all of that is locked inside the component and controlled centrally by the design studio.

This is governance through structure rather than policy. It does not require auditing templates to check whether someone changed the wrong font. It does not require training local users on brand rules they will forget. The template simply does not give them the option.

For design studio managers who support large field networks — retail chains, franchise groups, regional marketing teams — this is one of the most significant operational benefits of components.

---

## Campaign delivery with components

Consider how a typical promotional campaign plays out in a component-based studio versus a traditional one.

**Without components:** The design studio receives a brief for a six-product leaflet. A designer builds the template, laying out six product sections with all the design logic embedded in each. When the price of one product changes two days before print, the designer opens the template, finds the right text frame, updates the value, exports again. When the same campaign runs next month with six different products, the template is rebuilt or heavily edited.

**With components:** The product ad element is already a component in the library. The designer assembles the leaflet template in a fraction of the time — six instances of the component, each mapped to a set of product variables. When a price changes, the variable is updated in the data, not in the template. When the campaign runs next month with different products, the same template is reused with different variable values.

The design studio's work shifts from production to configuration. The template becomes infrastructure rather than a deliverable.

---

## Updating components after launch

When a component is updated and saved, every template that uses it reflects the change automatically. Local teams and operators do not need to do anything — the next time they open their template, it shows the updated component.

This makes a component library a continuous asset rather than a snapshot. The studio can improve component designs, fix issues, and update brand elements over time, with the confidence that those improvements reach every template immediately.

The practical implication for campaign planning: component updates and template updates are separate decisions. A component can be updated mid-campaign — for example to fix a layout issue — without the template itself needing to be touched.

---

## Building your component library

A component library does not need to be built all at once. The most effective approach is to start with the elements that appear most frequently in your current template work and build from there.

**A practical starting sequence:**

Start by auditing your existing templates for repeated elements — the design elements that appear in multiple templates or multiple times within a template. These are your highest-value candidates. Build the first two or three as components. Use them in the next campaign. The team will quickly see the value in practice.

From there, make it a habit: when a designer is about to build the same element they built last month, that element becomes a component instead.

Over time, the library grows into a set of reusable building blocks that cover most of your campaign needs. New templates become faster to assemble, brand updates become less disruptive, and local teams get materials more quickly.

---

## The studio team's new shape

Running a component-based studio changes what different roles focus on.

**Senior designers** own the component library. They define the design standards that get locked into each component — the typography choices, the layout rules, the brand logic. Their work has more reach: a decision they make in a component propagates to every template that uses it.

**Template designers** work faster. Building a template means selecting and configuring components, defining the variable structure, and mapping data. Less repetitive layout work; more focus on how the pieces fit together for a specific campaign.

**Local teams** get more autonomy within clearer guardrails. They can produce their own materials — filling in variables, selecting from allowed options — without needing to go back to HQ for every change. And because the design is protected inside the component, they cannot break the brand by accident.
